### Framework: Navigating the Bifurcation

**If you're a FOUNDER:**
- Raise when you can, not when you must
- The Series A gap is real—plan for 24-month runway minimum
- Consider emerging managers (more attention, faster decisions)
- Don't optimize for highest valuation; optimize for best partner

**If you're an LP:**
- Diversify across fund sizes (emerging managers outperform early stage)
- Accept that DPI will be delayed—don't panic-sell secondaries
- Maintain relationships with Fund I managers—they remember who backed them
- Expect 12-15 year cycles, not 10

**If you're a GP:**
- Right-size your fund to realistic exit markets
- A $50M fund needs a $500M exit; a $1B fund needs multiple $5B+ exits
- Focus on ownership percentage, not check size
- Build LP relationships during downturns when everyone else retreats

### Action Items

□ **This Week:** Audit your fund/portfolio against the health metrics above
□ **This Month:** Map your realistic exit universe (who acquires your companies?)
□ **This Quarter:** If raising, build relationships with emerging-manager-friendly LPs
□ **This Year:** Position for the 2026-2027 recovery, not today's trough

### Framework: Interpreting Fund Metrics

**TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In):**
- What it measures: Total value (realized + unrealized) divided by capital invested
- The catch: Unrealized value is based on marks, which can be optimistic
- How to use: Good directional indicator, but discount unrealized heavily

**DPI (Distributions to Paid-In):**
- What it measures: Cash returned divided by capital invested
- The catch: Low early in fund life is normal; low late is concerning
- How to use: The only metric that's "real money"

**IRR (Internal Rate of Return):**
- What it measures: Annualized return accounting for timing
- The catch: Easily manipulated by early small exits
- How to use: Compare across similar vintage funds

**For Founders:**

## Chapter 5: VC Fund Economics

### The Insight

Most people—including many founders—don't understand how venture fund economics actually work. The math explains VC behavior: why they need huge outcomes, why they pass on "good" deals, and why fund size determines strategy.

### The Data

**Basic Fund Economics**

```
FUND ECONOMICS 101: $100M FUND EXAMPLE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

FUND STRUCTURE:
├── Fund Size:            $100M committed by LPs
├── Management Fee:       2% annually × 10 years = $20M total
├── Carry (profit share): 20% of profits above 1x return
├── GP Commitment:        1-2% ($1-2M from the partners)
└── Fund Life:            10 years (+2 year extension option)

WHAT'S ACTUALLY INVESTABLE:
├── Total Committed:      $100M
├── Less: Mgmt Fees:      -$20M
├── Deployable Capital:   $80M
│
├── Typical Portfolio:    20-30 companies
├── Average Initial:      $2-3M per company
└── Reserves:             40-50% for follow-ons

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**The Return Math**

```
TO RETURN 3x NET TO LPs ($100M FUND):
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LPs invested:               $100M
LPs expect back (3x):       $300M net
Plus Management Fees:       +$20M (already taken)
Plus Carry (20% of $200M):  +$40M
═══════════════════════════════════════════
GROSS RETURNS NEEDED:       $360M

From $80M deployed, need $360M back = 4.5x GROSS multiple

BUT accounting for failures:
├── 50% of companies fail (return 0): $40M → $0
├── 30% return 1-2x (muddle):         $24M → $36M  
├── 20% are winners:                  $16M → Must return $324M
└── Winners need:                     20x+ average return

REALITY: You need a few 20-50x+ returns to make the fund work.
This is why VCs chase massive outcomes.
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Key Metrics Explained

```
VC METRICS CHEAT SHEET
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In)
├── Formula: (Realized + Unrealized) ÷ Capital Invested
├── Example: ($50M distributed + $100M marked) ÷ $80M = 1.88x
├── Use: Directional indicator of fund health
└── Caveat: Unrealized is based on marks (can be optimistic)

DPI (Distributions to Paid-In)
├── Formula: Cash Returned ÷ Capital Invested  
├── Example: $50M distributed ÷ $80M = 0.63x
├── Use: Only "real" metric (actual money back)
└── Caveat: Low early in fund life is normal

RVPI (Residual Value to Paid-In)
├── Formula: Unrealized Value ÷ Capital Invested
├── Example: $100M marked ÷ $80M = 1.25x
├── Use: Future potential (if marks are accurate)
└── Caveat: Marks can be very wrong

IRR (Internal Rate of Return)
├── Formula: Annualized return accounting for timing
├── Example: 25% net IRR is excellent
├── Use: Compare across vintages/strategies
└── Caveat: Can be gamed by early small exits

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Benchmarks by Fund Year

| Year | Expected TVPI | Expected DPI | Red Flag If |
|------|---------------|--------------|-------------|
| 1-2 | 0.8-1.1x | 0x | TVPI >1.5x (over-marking) |
| 3-4 | 1.1-1.5x | 0-0.2x | TVPI <1.0x (problems) |
| 5-6 | 1.5-2.0x | 0.2-0.5x | DPI still 0x |
| 7-8 | 2.0-2.5x | 0.5-1.5x | DPI <0.3x |
| 9-10 | 2.5-3.5x | 1.5-2.5x | Still mostly unrealized |

### Action Items

□ **LPs:** Understand the math before committing. Ask about realistic exit paths.
□ **GPs:** Right-size your fund. Bigger isn't better if exit math doesn't work.
□ **Founders:** Know your investor's fund size and what returns they need from you.
□ **Everyone:** Respect the power law. Most returns come from very few companies.

## Chapter 17: The DPI Dilemma

### Understanding LP Pressure

Limited partners are under intense pressure to show returns. This pressure flows downhill to GPs, who then make suboptimal decisions.

### The Math of Patience

```
EARLY EXIT vs. HOLD FOR MAXIMUM VALUE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

SCENARIO: $2M seed investment at $20M valuation (10% ownership)

PATH A: EARLY EXIT (YEAR 4)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Company acquired for $200M
• Your stake: 10% × $200M = $20M
• Return: 10x
• DPI contribution: $20M / $2M = 10x ✓

PATH B: HOLD FOR IPO (YEAR 8)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Dilution through Series A, B, C: 10% → 5%
• Company IPOs at $5B
• Your stake: 5% × $5B = $250M
• Return: 125x
• DPI contribution: $250M / $2M = 125x ✓✓✓

DIFFERENCE: 12.5x more value by waiting 4 more years

The problem: LPs demanding early DPI push VCs toward PATH A,
destroying the optionality that makes venture work.
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### When DPI Pressure Is Destructive

```
SCENARIOS WHERE DPI PRESSURE HURTS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

SCENARIO 1: PREMATURE SALE
• Company growing 50% YoY, on path to $1B+
• GP pressured for DPI, sells at $200M

• LP actually worse off

SCENARIO 2: RISK-AVERSE DEPLOYMENT
• Fund avoids bold bets (need safer paths to exit)

• Fund returns 2x instead of 5x
• "Safe" decisions aren't safe for returns

SCENARIO 3: FORCED SECONDARY
• GP sells position at 30% discount
• Quick DPI looks good
• Total return materially lower
• Trading dollars today for dollars tomorrow

SCENARIO 4: CONTINUATION FUND ABUSE
• GP moves mediocre assets to continuation fund

• Actual value hasn't changed
• Fees on fees

```

LP DUE DILIGENCE FRAMEWORK

└── Prior fund performance poor (if applicable)

PILLAR 2: INVESTMENT STRATEGY (20% weight)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Does the strategy make sense and is it executable?

EVALUATE:
├── Thesis clarity: Can they explain it in 2 sentences?
├── Market sizing: Is the opportunity real?
├── Entry point: What stage, what check size?
├── Ownership targets: Realistic for fund size?
├── Follow-on strategy: How much reserved?
├── Portfolio construction: Concentration vs. diversification

├── "We invest in great founders" (no real thesis)
├── Thesis doesn't match fund size
├── No clear edge in sourcing
├── Ownership targets unrealistic
├── Follow-on strategy unclear
└── Exit universe too narrow

QUESTIONS TO ASK:
• Why will founders choose you over established funds?
• Walk me through your ideal investment scenario
• How do you say no? What's a pass?
• What's your anti-portfolio (deals you passed on)?

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

PILLAR 3: TEAM (25% weight)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Can this team execute for 10+ years?

EVALUATE:
├── Partner dynamics: Do they work well together?
├── Complementary skills: Coverage of key functions
├── Decision-making: How do they decide? Consensus? Lead?
├── Succession: What if a partner leaves?
├── Incentive alignment: Carry split, GP commit
├── Support team: Operations, value-add capabilities
└── References: What do founders/co-investors say?

RED FLAGS:
├── Partners met recently (no working history)
├── All partners have same background
├── Unclear decision rights
├── Low GP commit (< 1%)
├── No plan for team growth
├── Bad references from founders

QUESTIONS TO ASK:
• Tell me about a time you disagreed on an investment
• What's each partner's specific role?
• What happens if one of you leaves?
• Who makes the final call on investments?

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

PILLAR 4: OPERATIONS (15% weight)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Can they run a fund professionally?

EVALUATE:
├── Fund administration: Quality service providers
├── Legal structure: Clean docs, market terms
├── Reporting: Frequency, quality, transparency
├── Valuation policy: How do they mark?
├── Compliance: Regulatory awareness
├── LP relations: Communication quality
└── Back-office: CFO, operations support

RED FLAGS:
├── Unknown/low-quality service providers
├── Non-market legal terms
├── Infrequent/poor-quality reporting
├── Aggressive marking policies
├── No operational support
├── Can't explain their own terms

QUESTIONS TO ASK:

• How do you value private companies?
• Who are your service providers?
• What's your LP communication cadence?

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

PILLAR 5: TERMS & ALIGNMENT (15% weight)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Are the economics aligned with LP interests?

STANDARD TERMS:
├── Management fee: 2% on committed (deployment), 2% on invested (harvest)
├── Carried interest: 20% over 1x preferred return
├── GP commit: 1-2% of fund size

└── Clawback: GP returns excess carry

RED FLAGS:
├── Management fee > 2.5%
├── No preferred return (hurdle)
├── GP commit < 1%
├── No key person clause
├── Excessive fund life
├── Non-standard waterfall

```

### The Rule of 40

```
UNDERSTANDING RULE OF 40
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

FORMULA:
Revenue Growth Rate (%) + Free Cash Flow Margin (%) ≥ 40%

EXAMPLES:
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Company A: 60% growth + (-20%) FCF = 40 ✓ (Growth mode)
Company B: 30% growth + 10% FCF = 40 ✓ (Balanced)
Company C: 15% growth + 25% FCF = 40 ✓ (Efficient)
Company D: 20% growth + 5% FCF = 25 ✗ (Underperforming)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHY IT MATTERS:
├── Balances growth and efficiency
├── Recognizes different valid strategies
├── Correlates with public market valuations
├── Useful benchmark across company types
└── Forces trade-off consciousness

CAVEATS:
├── Works best at scale ($10M+ ARR)
├── Earlier stage = prioritize growth side
├── Market conditions affect expectations
└── Rule of 50+ is elite

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Chapter 26: The a16z $15B Fund Analysis

### Can Mega-Funds Generate Venture Returns?

The announcement of Andreessen Horowitz's $15B fund sparked debate about whether venture returns are achievable at this scale. Here's the complete analysis.

### The Math

```
$15B FUND RETURN ANALYSIS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

FUND STRUCTURE (ESTIMATED):
├── Total Fund Size: $15B
├── Management Fees (10yr): ~$2B
├── Deployable Capital: ~$13B
├── GP Commit (2%): ~$300M
└── Target Net Multiple: 3x

TO RETURN 3x NET ($45B):
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LP Invested:              $15B
Target return to LPs:     $45B (3x)
Plus management fees:     +$2B
Plus carry (20%):         +$8B
─────────────────────────
GROSS REQUIRED:           ~$55B

From $13B deployed, need ~$55B returned = 4.2x gross multiple

HOW THEY GET THERE:
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Assume they deploy into 100-150 companies
Assume 50% fail completely
Assume 30% return 1-3x
Assume 20% are winners

For the math to work:
• Need multiple $50B+ exits
• Need maintained ownership through later rounds
• Need to hold through IPO and beyond (if stock appreciation)

EXAMPLE MATH:
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Databricks at $134B valuation
a16z ownership: ~5% (estimated)
a16z stake value: ~$6.7B

If Databricks IPOs at $200B:
a16z stake: ~$10B
= 67% of the way to fund returning 3x from ONE company

This is why concentration in mega-outcomes matters at scale.
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Chapter 29: Building for AI-Native Companies

### What Separates AI-Native from AI-Enhanced

Not all AI companies are created equal. AI-native companies build fundamentally different products than companies that add AI features to existing products.

### The Framework

```
AI-NATIVE vs. AI-ENHANCED
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

AI-ENHANCED (Adding AI to Existing Product):
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Product existed before AI
• AI improves specific features
• Core value prop remains the same
• AI is a feature, not the foundation
• Examples: Notion AI, Salesforce Einstein, Adobe Firefly

AI-NATIVE (Built From AI First):
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Product couldn't exist without AI
• AI is the core technology
• New workflows/capabilities
• Architecture designed for AI
• Examples: Harvey, Cursor, Jasper (early), Character.AI

VALUATION IMPLICATIONS:
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                    AI-Enhanced      AI-Native
Defensibility       Lower            Higher
Premium Multiple    1.2-1.5x         2-4x
Category Risk       Being featured   Commoditization
Best Case           Better product   New category
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Building Blocks of AI-Native Products

```
AI-NATIVE ARCHITECTURE CHECKLIST
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

DATA LAYER:
□ Proprietary data collection from Day 1
□ Data flywheel designed into product
□ Privacy/security architecture for AI data
□ Feedback loops that improve models
□ Data moat plan (how do you get data others can't?)

MODEL LAYER:
□ Start with best available models (don't build your own early)

□ Evaluation framework (how do you know if it's working?)

APPLICATION LAYER:
□ AI failures handled gracefully

□ Explanation/transparency for AI decisions
□ Confidence scoring (when is AI sure vs. uncertain?)
□ Audit trail for regulated industries

USER EXPERIENCE:
□ Designed for AI interaction (not retrofitted)

## Chapter 33: Building Your Investment Thesis

### The Insight

Every successful investor has a clear thesis—a framework for what they believe, where they focus, and why they'll win. Without it, you're gambling. This chapter helps you build yours.

### The Thesis Framework

```

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. WORLDVIEW
   └── What do you believe about the future that others don't?

2. FOCUS AREAS
   └── Where specifically will you invest? (Stage, sector, geo)

3. EDGE
   └── Why will you see and win the best deals?

4. EVALUATION CRITERIA
   └── What makes a company fundable in your framework?

5. VALUE-ADD
   └── What unique help can you provide founders?

6. PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION
   └── How many bets, at what sizes, with what reserves?

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Component 1: Worldview

```
DEVELOPING YOUR WORLDVIEW
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Answer these questions honestly:

MARKET BELIEFS:
├── Where is value being created that others underestimate?
├── What trends are real vs. hype?
├── Where does your experience give you unique insight?
└── What's obvious to you that isn't obvious to most VCs?

CONTRARIAN TEST:
├── A good thesis is non-consensus AND right
├── If everyone agrees with you, there's no edge
├── What do you believe that most investors would disagree with?

EXAMPLE WORLDVIEWS:
├── "Vertical AI will outperform horizontal plays"
├── "B2B marketplaces are underinvested vs. B2C"
├── "Climate tech is infrastructure, not charity"
├── "The best founders are domain experts, not serial entrepreneurs"

YOUR WORLDVIEW SHOULD BE:
├── Specific enough to guide decisions
├── Contrarian enough to create opportunity
├── Defensible with evidence and reasoning
└── Aligned with your actual expertise

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Component 2: Focus Areas

```
DEFINING YOUR FOCUS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

STAGE FOCUS:
├── Pre-seed: $250K-$1.5M checks, highest risk, earliest signal
├── Seed: $500K-$3M checks, product risk, team-heavy
├── Series A: $5M-$15M checks, PMF validation, scaling risk

├── Broad: "Enterprise software" (too wide for real edge)
├── Medium: "Developer tools" (workable)
├── Sharp: "Infrastructure for ML ops" (real expertise possible)
│
└── RULE: Sharp enough to develop pattern recognition.

GEOGRAPHY:
├── SF/NYC = Highest deal flow, most competition
├── Secondary hubs = Less competition, harder to find
├── Remote-first = Global access, harder relationship building
│
└── RULE: Your network determines your geography.

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

IDENTIFYING YOUR EDGE

EDGE AUDIT:

### Common LP Mistakes

```
LP MISTAKES TO AVOID
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

MISTAKE #1: CHASING HOT FUNDS
├── Problem: Best funds are closed or capacity-constrained
├── Reality: Getting into "top" funds often means catching them late
├── Fix: Focus on next generation, not last generation's winners

MISTAKE #2: OVER-RELYING ON PAST RETURNS
├── Problem: VC returns take 7-10 years; data is always stale
├── Reality: Today's Fund V may look nothing like Fund II
├── Fix: Focus on team, strategy evolution, and attribution

MISTAKE #3: IGNORING FUND SIZE GROWTH
├── Problem: Strategy that works at $100M often fails at $500M
├── Reality: Returns typically degrade as funds scale
├── Fix: Ask hard questions about capacity constraints

MISTAKE #4: NOT RE-UPPING CONSISTENTLY
├── Problem: Skip vintage years, miss best opportunities
├── Reality: Returns compound over time; gaps hurt
├── Fix: Commit to steady-state program, don't time the market

MISTAKE #5: UNDERWEIGHTING EMERGING MANAGERS
├── Problem: "Safe" allocation to mega-funds only
├── Reality: Emerging managers consistently outperform
├── Fix: Allocate 25-35% to emerging managers systematically

MISTAKE #6: INSUFFICIENT DILIGENCE ON ATTRIBUTION
├── Problem: Returns attributed to "the fund" not specific people
├── Reality: Key partners leave; returns often leave with them
├── Fix: Map every winner to the specific partner who sourced it

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Action Items

**For family offices new to VC:**
1. Start with 2-3 diversified funds across stages
2. Commit to 3-year deployment minimum
3. Budget for 10+ year illiquidity
4. Consider emerging manager allocator as onramp

**For institutional LPs:**
1. Audit portfolio for emerging manager exposure
2. Review fund size growth vs. return trajectory for each GP
3. Implement systematic attribution tracking
4. Set vintage year targets, not opportunistic deployment

**For all LPs:**
1. Reference check aggressively (founders, co-investors, existing LPs)
2. Ask for attribution data on every return
3. Track manager consistency across funds
4. Build relationships before you need to deploy

## Chapter 35: Negotiating Term Sheets

### The Insight

Term sheets aren't just legal documents—they're the foundation of your investor relationship and can dramatically impact outcomes. Understanding what matters (and what doesn't) prevents regrettable decisions.

### The Data

```
WHAT FOUNDERS FOCUS ON VS. WHAT MATTERS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

                    FOUNDER FOCUS     ACTUAL IMPACT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Valuation           ████████████████  ████████ (High but overrated)
Board seats         ████              ████████████ (Underrated)
Pro-rata rights     ██                ████████████ (Underrated)
Liquidation pref    ████              ████████████████ (Very high)
Anti-dilution       ████              ████████ (High in down round)
Drag-along          ██                ████████████ (High at exit)
Information rights  █                 ████ (Low)
ROFR                ██                ████████ (High for liquidity)
Founder vesting     ████              ████████████ (High if issues)
Option pool         ████████          ████████ (Moderate)

PATTERN: Founders optimize for valuation.
REALITY: Governance and exit terms matter more long-term.
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Term Sheet Components Explained

```
ECONOMICS VS. CONTROL
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

ECONOMICS (Who gets paid what):
├── Valuation (pre-money, post-money)
├── Liquidation preference (1x, 2x, participating)
├── Anti-dilution (broad-based weighted average)
├── Dividends (rare in early stage)
└── Option pool (size and placement)

CONTROL (Who decides what):
├── Board composition
├── Protective provisions (veto rights)
├── Drag-along rights
├── Information rights
├── Voting rights

LIQUIDITY (How you get money out):
├── ROFR (right of first refusal)
├── Co-sale rights
├── Registration rights
├── Redemption rights

GENERAL PRINCIPLE:
├── Seed: Economics matter, control minimal
├── Series A: Balance of economics and control
├── Series B+: Control provisions become more important
└── Late stage: All terms tighten, scrutinize everything

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### What to Negotiate Hard

```
NEGOTIATION PRIORITIES BY STAGE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

SEED STAGE: FOCUS HERE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
├── Valuation cap (reasonable range: $6-15M pre)
├── Pro-rata rights (investors will push; reasonable to grant)
├── Board: Keep at 1-2 founders only, no investor seats
├── Side letters: Avoid or limit them
│
│   DON'T FIGHT ABOUT:
│   └── Most things—SAFE/convertible is founder-friendly by design

SERIES A: FIGHT FOR THESE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
├── Board composition: 2 founders + 1 investor (not 2+2+1)
├── Protective provisions: Limit list to truly important items
├── Option pool: Push for post-money carve-out
├── Anti-dilution: Broad-based weighted average (not narrow/full)
│
│   WATCH OUT FOR:
│   ├── Participating preferred (rare but very bad)
│   ├── >1x liquidation preference
│   ├── Cumulative dividends
│   └── Super pro-rata rights

SERIES B+: SCRUTINIZE THESE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
├── Drag-along: What thresholds trigger it?
├── Board expansion: Resist adding more investor seats
├── Pay-to-play: Understand implications
├── Redemption: Avoid if possible
│
│   BY THIS STAGE:
│   └── You need a great lawyer. Every clause matters.

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Red Flags in Term Sheets

```
TERM SHEET RED FLAGS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

SERIOUS RED FLAGS (Walk away or negotiate hard):
├── Participating preferred: Investor gets 1x back PLUS pro-rata
├── >1x liquidation preference: 2x+ means heavy downside stack
├── Full ratchet anti-dilution: Destroys founders in down round
├── Cumulative dividends: Liability that grows over time
├── Redemption rights at investor option: Forced liquidity trigger
├── Board majority to investors early: Loss of control
└── Unusual vesting resets: Watch for acceleration elimination

MODERATE FLAGS (Negotiate or understand fully):
├── Narrow-based weighted average anti-dilution
├── Broad protective provisions (veto list too long)
├── Super pro-rata rights (>2x investment right)
├── Side letters you haven't seen
├── Unclear treatment of option pool
└── Ambiguous founder vesting terms

MINOR FLAGS (Know what you're agreeing to):
├── Standard observer rights
├── Information rights
├── ROFR with reasonable mechanics
├── Standard registration rights
└── Normal indemnification

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Negotiation Tactics

```
TERM SHEET NEGOTIATION PLAYBOOK
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

BEFORE NEGOTIATION:
├── Know your BATNA (best alternative to negotiated agreement)
├── Understand market terms (talk to other founders)
├── Have multiple term sheets if possible
├── Know your priorities—what you'll fight for vs. concede
└── Get a great lawyer who does VC deals

DURING NEGOTIATION:
├── Valuation: Justify with comps, traction, market
├── Lead with data, not emotion
├── Package trades: "We'll accept X if you'll give Y"
├── Don't fight every clause—pick battles
└── Time is your friend early, your enemy late

TACTICS THAT WORK:
├── "That's outside market norms—here's the data"
├── "Can you help me understand why you need that?"
├── "We have another term sheet that doesn't include this"
├── "Our lawyer flagged this as unusual—what's the intent?"
└── Create urgency without bluffing

TACTICS THAT BACKFIRE:
├── Bluffing about competing offers (they check)
├── Threatening to walk on minor issues
├── Being adversarial instead of collaborative
├── Involving lawyers before building rapport
└── Rushing to close without understanding terms

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

### Action Items

**Before signing any term sheet:**
1. Read every word—don't rely on summaries
2. Ask founders who took money from this investor about surprises
3. Model the payout in various exit scenarios (down, flat, up)
4. Understand your board dynamics and veto implications
5. Sleep on it—24-48 hours minimum for any significant term

**Build leverage by:**
1. Running a tight process with multiple interested parties
2. Having strong metrics that speak for themselves
3. Being willing to walk away (and meaning it)
4. Understanding exactly what you're trading off
